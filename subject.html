DNA

DNA stands for deoxyribonucleic acid and it holds all the instructions necessary for a living organism to function. DNA is found inside the nucleus of a eukaryotic cell, but for a prokaryote, it is found in the cytoplasm. DNA is in the shape of a double helix, with a pentose sugar and a phosphate as the sides of the "ladder" and the nitrogenous bases as the "rungs". There are four nitrogenous bases: adenine(A), thymine(T), guanine(G), and cytosine(C). A and G are purines, while T and C are pyrimidines, and opposites go together. A pairs with T, while C pairs with G. A and T are bonded by two hydrogen bonds, while C and G are bonded by three hydrogen bonds, so C and G have a strong bond than A and T. When undergoing mitosis, DNA replication happens in three steps: initiation, elongation, and termination. Immune System

The immune system is a complex network of cells that communicate with each other to fend off the body from foreign substances, such as bacteria and viruses. The white blood cells, aka leukocytes, are the main cells that fight off pathogens. Phagocytes are one of the first white blood cells that help fight against bacteria that have penetrated through the first line of defense, or in other words, the innate immunity, by swallowing them and breaking them down. Innate immunity is immunity that a person is born with, such as skin, sweat, tears, and mucus. Another type of white blood cells are the lymphocytes. There are two kinds, B cells and T cells. Specific B cells will become active and produce specific antibodies that will help fight against a specific type of pathogen. Other B cells become memory B cells that will remember the antigens on the specific pathogen, just in case the body is infected with the same pathogen at a later point in time. T cells are killer cells that will kill infected cells. Like B cells, they are also specific and specific T cells will be activated according to the type of pathogen that enters the body. Mitosis

Mitosis is the process of a cell splitting into two genetically identical cells. Mitosis has five steps: prophase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase, and cytokinesis. When the cell starts mitosis, it has already replicated its DNA and its organelles in interphase. In prophase, the DNA starts to condense again as the nucleus dissolves and the spindle fibers start to form. In prometaphase, a phase between prophase and metaphase, the spindle fibers push the chromosomes towards the center. In metaphase, the chromosomes are all lined up in the center of the cell. In anaphase, the spindle fibers pull sister chromatids to opposite ends of the cell. Kinetochores, which are attached to the sister chromatids, pull them to opposite ends by breaking down the spindle fibers and pulling the chromatids along. In telophase, spindle fibers disappear, and the chromosomes start to decondense as the nucleus reforms. In cytokinesis, the cleavage furrow or the cell plate, depending on whether the cell is an animal cell or a plant cell, forms in the middle of the cell and the cell splits into two identical daughter cells and they go back into the cell cycle. In the cell cycle, there are checkpoints that check for any errors in the cell. If a cell fails to meet the checkpoint, then the cell tries to fix the error and continue along the cell cycle.
